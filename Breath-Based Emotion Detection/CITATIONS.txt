Measurement citations used in this project
==========================================

This project uses a transparent measurement-based heuristic instead of a
trained emotion-recognition model. The app measures microphone loudness in
dBFS, averages the adjusted loudness over a 32-second window, and maps that
window average to broad emotion labels.

Measurement applied in the code:

- below -55 dBFS: very quiet / unclear
- -55 to -42 dBFS: quiet / calm
- -42 to -30 dBFS: moderate / active
- -30 to 0 dBFS: loud / stressed or excited
- prediction window: 32 seconds

Important limitation:

The papers below support using vocal loudness, energy, and intensity as
emotion-related acoustic cues, and they support using time windows for signal
measurement. They do not validate these exact dBFS threshold numbers as a
clinical or universal emotion detector. The thresholds are simple project
heuristics for the selected microphone and input gain.


Digital decibel measurement
---------------------------

1. Library of Congress, Federal Agencies Digital Guidelines Initiative.
   Decibels relative to full scale.
   URL: https://www.digitizationguidelines.gov/term.php?term=decibelsrelativetofullscale

   Used for: defining the app's dBFS measurement scale. The code computes
   20 * log10(RMS amplitude), where 0 dBFS is digital full scale and quieter
   input has negative values. These readings are relative to the computer's
   microphone signal, not calibrated real-world dBA sound-pressure levels.


Decibels, loudness, and vocal emotion cues
------------------------------------------

2. Banse, R., & Scherer, K. R. (1996). Acoustic profiles in vocal emotion
   expression. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 70(3), 614-636.
   DOI: 10.1037/0022-3514.70.3.614
   URL: https://www.columbia.edu/~rmk7/HC/HC_Readings/Scherer.pdf

   Used for: supporting the idea that measurable vocal acoustic parameters,
   including intensity-related cues, differ across emotional expressions. This
   is the closest citation for correlating voice tone/loudness measurements with
   emotion-related categories.

3. Juslin, P. N., & Laukka, P. (2003). Communication of emotions in vocal
   expression and music performance: Different channels, same code?
   Psychological Bulletin, 129(5), 770-814.
   DOI: 10.1037/0033-2909.129.5.770
   URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12956543/

   Used for: reviewing many studies showing that emotion in voice is expressed
   through acoustic cues. This supports using voice intensity/loudness as one
   cue for broad arousal differences, while reminding us that loudness alone is
   not enough for reliable emotion classification.

4. Ververidis, D., & Kotropoulos, C. (2006). Emotional speech recognition:
   Resources, features, and methods. Speech Communication, 48(9), 1162-1181.
   DOI: 10.1016/j.specom.2006.04.003
   URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167639306000422

   Used for: connecting speech emotion recognition with measurable acoustic
   features, including speech-signal intensity. This supports the app's choice
   to use a loudness/energy measurement as a simple emotion-related cue.

5. El Ayadi, M., Kamel, M. S., & Karray, F. (2011). Survey on speech emotion
   recognition: Features, classification schemes, and databases. Pattern
   Recognition, 44(3), 572-587.
   DOI: 10.1016/j.patcog.2010.09.020
   URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0031320310004619

   Used for: supporting the use of acoustic features such as energy and pitch in
   speech emotion recognition. The app uses only the energy/loudness side of
   this feature family.

6. Eyben, F., Scherer, K. R., Schuller, B. W., Sundberg, J., Andre, E.,
   Busso, C., Devillers, L. Y., Epps, J., Laukka, P., Narayanan, S. S., &
   Truong, K. P. (2016). The Geneva Minimalistic Acoustic Parameter Set
   (GeMAPS) for Voice Research and Affective Computing. IEEE Transactions on
   Affective Computing, 7(2), 190-202.
   DOI: 10.1109/TAFFC.2015.2457417
   URL: https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1109/TAFFC.2015.2457417

   Used for: showing that standardized voice feature sets for affective
   computing include loudness-related measurements. This supports measuring
   voice loudness as a legitimate input feature, even though this project uses a
   much simpler rule-based threshold method.


32-second emotion detection window
----------------------------------

7. Karlen, W., Raman, S., Ansermino, J. M., & Dumont, G. A. (2013).
   Multiparameter respiratory rate estimation from the photoplethysmogram.
   IEEE Transactions on Biomedical Engineering, 60(7), 1946-1953.
   DOI: 10.1109/TBME.2013.2246160
   URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23399950/

   Used for: supporting the idea of using a 32-second window when measuring
   breathing-related signals. This project uses a 32-second window to average
   microphone dBFS values before assigning an emotion label.

8. Jarchi, D., Salvi, D., Tarassenko, L., & Clifton, D. A. (2018). Validation
   of Instantaneous Respiratory Rate Using Reflectance PPG from Different Body
   Positions. Sensors, 18(11), 3705.
   DOI: 10.3390/s18113705
   URL: https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/18/11/3705

   Used for: documenting that respiratory-rate measurement methods often use
   windowed segments with a minimum of about 32 seconds. The app applies the
   same window length as a stable period for deciding the emotion category from
   averaged decibel readings.
