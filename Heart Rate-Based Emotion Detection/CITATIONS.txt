HEALTHY RESTING HEART-RATE REFERENCE

Maake, Mohale Gift, Kotsedi Daniel Monyeki, and Machoene Derrick Sekgala.
"The Relationship Between Resting Heart Rate and Cardiovascular Risk Factors
Among Adults Aged 18 Years and Above in the Rural Ellisras Population:
Ellisras Longitudinal Study." Journal of Cardiovascular Development and
Disease 12, no. 11 (2025): 428.
https://doi.org/10.3390/jcdd12110428

Used in bpm_gui.py for the adult resting-heart-rate reference bands:
  Low: below 60 BPM
  Typical: 60-100 BPM
  High: above 100 BPM

The peer-reviewed study categorizes adult resting heart rate as bradycardia
when below 60 BPM, normal from 60-100 BPM, and tachycardia when above 100 BPM.
Heart-rate measurements were taken after participants had been seated for at
least five minutes. The study also reports associations between resting heart
rate and several cardiovascular and metabolic factors. In bpm_gui.py, these
categories are used only as general reference bands and not as medical
diagnoses.


HEART-RATE-BASED EMOTION/AROUSAL HEURISTIC

Measurement applied in bpm_gui.py:
  Below 60 BPM: Low activation / possibly calm
  60-80 BPM: Typical activation / emotion unclear
  81-100 BPM: Elevated activation / possibly active, stressed, or excited
  Above 100 BPM: High activation / possibly stressed or excited

Important limitation:

These exact BPM boundaries are transparent project heuristics built around the
adult resting-heart-rate bands above; they are not clinically or universally
validated emotion thresholds. Heart rate reflects physiological arousal and
can change with emotion, but it is also affected by exercise, posture,
medication, fitness, illness, temperature, and individual baseline. Different
emotions can produce overlapping cardiac responses. The GUI therefore uses
"possibly" and "emotion unclear" and must not be treated as a diagnostic or a
reliable detector of a specific emotion. A personalized baseline, heart-rate
variability, and additional signals would be needed for stronger inference.

Academic support for heart rate as an emotion-related signal:

1. Kreibig, Sylvia D. "Autonomic Nervous System Activity in Emotion: A
   Review." Biological Psychology 84, no. 3 (2010): 394-421.
   https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biopsycho.2010.03.010

   Used for: reviewing emotion-related autonomic patterns, including changes
   in heart rate, while showing that responses vary across emotions and study
   conditions. It supports broad arousal-related interpretation, not the exact
   BPM cutoffs used by this project.

2. Rainville, Pierre, Antoine Bechara, Nasir Naqvi, and Antonio R. Damasio.
   "Basic Emotions Are Associated with Distinct Patterns of Cardiorespiratory
   Activity." International Journal of Psychophysiology 61, no. 1 (2006):
   5-18.
   https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijpsycho.2005.10.024

   Used for: experimental evidence that induced basic emotions are associated
   with differing heart-rate and respiratory patterns. The study supports
   cardiac activity as an emotion-related feature but does not establish fixed
   universal BPM thresholds for individual emotions.

3. Sayed Ismail, Sharifah Noor Masidayu, Nor Azlina Ab. Aziz, Siti Zainab
   Ibrahim, and Mohd Saberi Mohamad. "A Systematic Review of Emotion
   Recognition Using Cardio-Based Signals." ICT Express 10, no. 1 (2024):
   156-183.
   https://doi.org/10.1016/j.icte.2023.09.001

   Used for: documenting the use of ECG and PPG cardiac signals in automatic
   emotion-recognition research and the importance of signal features and
   classification methods beyond a single instantaneous BPM value.
